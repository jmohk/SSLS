Smart Street Light System

1.1 Identifying the problem.

In today's modern world, urban environments demand a well-planned infrastructure that is both energy-efficient and cost-effective. One effective solution is the integration of a smart street light system, which not only reduces energy consumption but also enhances safety and accessibility in urban areas. Street lighting is a core infrastructure piece in urban and semiurban cities. It provides several advantages such as improving safety for drivers and pedestrians. Nowadays, street lighting accounts for about 13-14% of the world’s electricity annual production, and the market is continuously growing. It is expected that by 2027, there will be about 363 million streetlights around the world. Consequently, enormous energy is consumed by the streetlights, which makes it imperative to work on solutions to reduce street light consumption. There are some countries that have implemented this project in cities and achieved a good result, it presented in energy consumption. And from the countries that used smart street light system are [China – Taiwan – USA – Japan – Korea – Sweden – Germany] Individually we will review the plans of these countries and the enforcement of this system to transform the street to smart saving the electrical energy and reduction of using the sailor or gas. While the features of smart streetlights depend on the specific technology used by city planners, examples of common functionality include the following:

  • Dynamic lighting controls based on movement detection.
  • Environmental and weather monitoring.
  • Digital signage that can be updated as needed, such as parking regulations or accident alerts.
  • Parking management, such as alerting officials of illegally parked vehicles or drivers of open spaces.
  • Extended cellular and wireless communications.

    • Implementing smart streetlight systems offers the following advantages:

  • Reduced energy cost and usage with flexible dimming controls.
  • Protect children because it is programmed to automatically turn off the lights in the event of rain or bad weather.
  • Increased pedestrian satisfaction through improved safety measures.
  • Lowered repair and maintenance costs with the monitoring software.
  • Reduced carbon emissions and light pollution.
  • Increased lamp life and shorter response times to outages.
  • Improved architectural planning based on real traffic patterns and insights.
  • Increased revenue opportunities, such as leasing poles for digital signage or other services.

    2.1 Problem Definition

    There are many properties of streetlight bulbs that we can modify to reduce electricity consumption and environmental pollution. However, this could lead to high-cost solutions, such as replacing the old gas bulbs, and would require a new infrastructure system to adapt to the new bulbs. Let’s consider less cost-effective solutions where technology plays a vital role. The question is, how can technology effectively manage this issue? Despite the type of street bulb, gas used, and electricity source, we will control the timing of these bulbs. This means we can create a system that gives orders to the street bulbs regarding when to be on/off and how many bulbs should be sufficient for a given street (considering each street's unique conditions). Our system adapts two modes to address this issue, and at the end of this project, we can compare these two modes numerically to determine which mode was more effective and how much we can improve the system.

    The first mode is the Manual Mode: where we provide the system with instructions on when to turn lights on/off regularly and how many bulbs should be on in a street, based on our (human) predictions of crowd and weather conditions.

    The second mode, which we like to call the Automated Mode: uses a machine learning algorithm known as the linear regression model. This mode uses a pre-trained dataset to predict the optimal amount of light needed based on multiple factors. It doesn't only consider the number of cars in the street but also scans weather conditions, recognizes the time, and identifies the current season of the year. Based on these factors, it determines how much light that street needs.

    Let’s dive deeper into these modes to discuss how they work and how we can represent this virtually to understand their mechanisms.

    2.2 Manual Mode

    The Manual mode simulates the traditional approach that electricity stations use to manage the streetlight system. It involves a basic on/off switch, which, in modern times, is not physically controlled by a human. Instead, it requires instructing the machine to turn on the lights from time x1 to time x2. This process is straightforward and is primarily based on darkness, with no other factors affecting the decision to turn the lights on or off.

    The issue with this mode is that it consumes electricity regularly, regardless of whether it is needed. There are days when the off-time interval coincides with rainy and cloudy weather, requiring a highly efficient light even though it is not the scheduled on-time interval.

    Our system replicates this lighting system methodology by setting when the light should be on and when it should be turned off, irrespective of the number of cars on the street and the prevailing weather conditions.

    Now you might ask yourself “why did they make two modes why not the automated only and for sure it will be better?” The answer is simple and easy, let’s split it into these points:

    1. This is a machine learning model (Lenier regression), the better way to prove its efficiency is to compare it with the mechanism that we have been using for decades.
    2. It’s important to see both modes right to each other so we can compare the numbers easily and simply say this mode is better or consume less electricity than the other and see simply how much each mode costs.

    2.3 Automated Mode

    Automated Mode is where we can say the technology magic happens, turning on or off light is now the machine decision. Using modern machine learning algorithms like Linear regression model is a good option in our case, let me tell you why but first what is Linear regression?

    Linear regression is an algorithm that provides a linear relationship between an independent variable and a dependent variable to predict the outcome of future events. It is a statistical method used in data science and machine learning for predictive analysis. It uses the relationship between the data-points to draw a straight line through all of them.

    In the simplest linear regression equation, Yi = f(Xi, β) + ei.

    • Yi represents the dependent variable, in our case, it is the percentage of how many light bulbs should be on for the street with its specific conditions.
    • Xi represents the independent variable which contains the many features that affect the decision that predicts how many light bulbs we need. The used features here are time, seasons of the year, rain, fog, and the number of cars.

    2.3.1 Time

    A straightforward explanation for time being a factor (feature) is that we don't need lights when the sun is up, and we require lights at night. Therefore, time is a simple yet crucial factor in this context.

    2.3.2 Year Season

    Considering the year season here is linked to the time factor, as mentioned before, we use time to determine when the sun is rising and when it is sunset time. There is a difference in sunrise time between summer and winter. Although it might be a small difference in calculations, it becomes a significant value in our equations.

    2.3.3 Rain & Fog

    These factors are considered as weather conditions that affect the visibility for cars on the streets. Therefore, we should keep them in mind when making decisions about the optimal amount of light needed for the street.

    2.3.4 No. of Vehicles

    Cars play a crucial role as the elements our system serves, and determining the number of cars in the street influences the model's decision for lighting the street. Additionally, cars are considered a street attribute; for example, Street X has a maximum capacity of 50 cars. Detecting these cars will be a process of computer vision, it is a field of artificial intelligence (AI) that enables computers and systems to derive meaningful information from digital images, videos and other visual inputs, our model is trained to see moving objects and detect which of them are cars and count their number to use as an input in our linear equation to predict the optimal percentage of lights needed. Our computer vision process will rely on a technology called OpenCV, it is a library of programming functions mainly for real-time computer vision. Originally developed by Intel, the library is cross-platform and licensed as free and open-source software under Apache License.
